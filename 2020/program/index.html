Dr. David Rothschild, Microsoft Research

Title: Real News is the Problem

Abstract:There is a lot of concern about how the news ecosystem affects public opinion, political polarization, and democracy. Most of the research and public debate focuses on overtly fake material, particularly as it is distributed through social media. But, the vast majority of news consumption is actual produced by mainstream media, and the most common mode of consumption is television. Meanwhile, many key demographic groups consume almost no news at all. Concerns about lack of information or misinformation should focus on where people actual consume (or don’t consume) news, and the product they are actually consuming: mainstream media.

Speaker: Prof. Jennifer Ann Golbeck, University of Maryland

Title: Fake Like Bots: How they manipulate and how we can detect them

Abstract:Bots do not behave naturally. While they can emulate human behavior in some senses, there are others they cannot control. We can detect this in many ways, including in patterns of social interaction. Identifying these atypical patterns is an important and effective component of a cohesive detection strategy. For fake like bots - bots that simply like or share posts without commenting - detection and elimination is especially important because of the effect they can have on the perceived trustworthiness or importance of content.

Speaker:Dr. Vasyl Pihur, Snap

Title: A stochastic model to estimate the abuse prevalence on a social media platform

Abstract:To understand the prevalence of something, one does not need to identify every element individually and count them. It is rather impossible to do so in most cases. Often, it is much easier to perform this estimation "anonymously", meaning we can estimate the size or cardinality of a set, without knowing what is exactly in it. There are several major techniques and approaches that are based on statistical estimation and sampling theory that allow for this indirect measurement. We will be taking one of these approaches in this work to understand the prevalence of offensive interactions on Snapchat, as well as their reach, in terms of how many users they have affected.

Speaker:Dr. Aude Hofleitner, Facebook Core Data Science

Title: Temporal Interaction EmbeddingS (TIES): Incorporating context for finding abuse at scale

Abstract:Machine learning models for detecting integrity issues have made significant progress thanks to research in natural language processing or computer vision. However, using content alone may not always be sufficient (or even available) for accurate predictions, and these algorithms still need to be complemented by hundreds of hand-engineered features aiming to capture user behavior. Producing these features is laborious, requires deep domain expertise, and may not capture all of the most important information about the entity being classified. In this talk we present an alternative approach: instead of relying on content alone or hand-crafting features, we distill the entire sequence of interactions involving the entity into a low-dimensional feature vector (or “embedding”) that can be input into a classifier for any prediction task. Our algorithm, TIES (Temporal Interaction EmbeddingS), is a supervised deep learning model that captures static features around each interaction source and target, as well as temporal features of the interaction sequence. In our implementation at Facebook, we found that TIES significantly improves existing classifiers for several prediction tasks involving an entity interacting with other objects online, and that our approach is particularly valuable in situations where context is essential to prediction (e.g. hate speech). We also expect TIES to provide additional value when content is not available (e.g., encrypted messaging), or when we want to make predictions before the harm is done (e.g., while a bad actor is searching for victims).

Bio:Aude Hofleitner is a manager and research scientist on the Core Data Science team at Facebook. Her research interests lie in the fields of machine learning, data mining, and graph representation learning. She received a PhD in electrical engineering and computer science from the University of California, Berkeley, where she studied statistical models of queuing networks using mobile data under the supervision of Alexandre Bayen and Pieter Abbeel. Before that, she received both bachelor’s and master’s degrees in applied mathematics at the École Polytechnique, France. She also received a PhD and a master’s in transportation engineering from École des Ponts ParisTech.
